# The Companion / Squire — An Addition to the Hero's Journey

> **Purpose:** Companion document to *The Hero's Journey — Frameworks Reference*. This file isolates the companion/squire archetype work: how the standard frameworks treat the companion, where they underdevelop it, what functions the companion actually performs, and case studies (Samwise Gamgee, Sancho Panza, and the broader literary tradition).
>
> **Why a separate file:** The standard frameworks (Campbell, Vogler, Harmon, Murdock) center the hero. The companion is either implicit (Campbell), one archetype among eight (Vogler), or absent as a discrete category (Harmon, Murdock). Treating the companion as its own object of study reveals patterns the hero-centered models compress or miss.
>
> **Last updated:** March 2026

## Where the Frameworks Place the Companion

A quick map of how each major framework handles (or fails to handle) the companion:

| Framework | Treatment of Companion |
|-----------|----------------------|
| **Campbell** | No discrete archetype. Companions appear inside the *Road of Trials* as "helpers" giving "magical aid." Companionship is a stage moment, not a category. |
| **Vogler** | Formalized as the **Ally** — one of eight archetypes. The shortest archetype chapter in *The Writer's Journey*. Defined functionally (sounding board, scout, comic relief, audience surrogate) rather than psychologically. |
| **Harmon** | No archetype layer at all. The Story Circle is purely structural. Companions, if present, live inside steps 3–5 (Go, Search, Find). |
| **Murdock** | No companion archetype. The Heroine's Journey is an internal one; relational figures appear as the Mother, the Wounded Masculine, etc., not as companions. |
| **Propp** | The **Magical Helper** is one of his seven *dramatis personae*. Propp gives the companion its most structurally precise role: a helper "may accomplish everything" while the hero supplies intention and direction. |
| **Pearson** | The **Caregiver** archetype captures part of the companion function. But Pearson's archetypes attach to the protagonist's own psyche; the companion-as-other-person is not isolated. |

**The pattern:** every framework gestures at the companion, but only Propp (structurally) and Vogler (briefly) treat it as a category. This is the gap the rest of this document fills.

## The Companion Archetype

The companion archetype — variously called the **Helper** (Campbell), the **Ally** (Vogler), the **Magical Helper** (Propp), and the **Caregiver** (Pearson) — is one of storytelling's oldest and most essential figures.

Campbell did not name a discrete "companion" archetype but referenced "helpers" who give "magical aid" along the Road of Trials. Vogler formalized this as the **Ally**, defined as characters who help the hero through changes — sidekicks, buddies, confidants who advise through transitions.

### The Loyal Retainer / Squire Sub-Type

The squire is traditionally someone "training to become a knight," but in modern storytelling the term generalizes to any companion whose **service is structured partly by class, duty, or hierarchy** — yet whose moral agency becomes decisive. The squire is often more beloved by audiences precisely because they are closer to the "common person" than the visionary or semi-divine master they serve.

This archetype's defining characteristic is **loyalty as an existential commitment, not a personality trait**. It has a shadow side: the companion can become blindly loyal to the point of danger, possessive, or consumed by unfavorable self-comparison to the hero. This shadow is precisely what Tolkien identified in Sam's hostility toward Gollum.

## Companion Functions

All source analyses converge on a consistent set of functions the companion performs:

| Function | How It Works in the Journey |
|----------|---------------------------|
| **Emotional Support / Regulation** | Absorbs panic, doubt, and grief; sustains endurance through the Road of Trials. Keeps the hero functional when despair threatens to end the quest. |
| **Moral Compass** | Voices ethical stakes, challenges rationalizations, anchors the hero to ordinary life and its values. The "conscience" function (Enkidu to Gilgamesh, Sam to Frodo). |
| **Foil and Contrast Engine** | Makes the hero's qualities visible through difference — ideal vs. real, noble vs. common, reckless vs. prudent (Quixote/Sancho, Holmes/Watson). |
| **Helper / Competence Extender** | Supplies knowledge, tools, translation, connections, or brute capability the hero lacks. The "magical helper" logic from Propp. |
| **Mentor-in-Training** | Begins as subordinate, becomes capable of independent action, sometimes inherits leadership. The classic squire-to-knight trajectory. |
| **Audience Surrogate / Narrator** | Gives the audience access to the hero; controls admiration or skepticism via focalization. Watson narrates Holmes; Sam voices the reader's wonder and fear. |
| **Humanizer** | Gives the hero someone to talk to, allowing internal conflicts to be expressed through dialogue rather than exposition. |
| **Loss Catalyst** | The companion's loss or sacrifice catalyzes the hero's deepest transformation. Enkidu's death drives Gilgamesh's quest; Patroclus's death breaks Achilles' pride. |

## Companions Across Literary History

| Companion | Hero / Text | Primary Functions | Narrative Significance |
|-----------|------------|-------------------|----------------------|
| **Enkidu** | Gilgamesh (c. 2100 BCE) | Equal companion; civilizing force; moral mirror; loss catalyst | First known literary companion. Created by gods to temper a tyrant; his death reshapes the hero's entire purpose. |
| **Patroclus** | Achilles (*Iliad*, 8th c. BCE) | Emotional anchor; moral pressure; catalyst via loss | Companion's borrowed armor and battlefield death constitute the *Iliad*'s turning point. |
| **Hanuman** | Rama (*Ramayana*) | Helper; messenger; super-competent agent; devoted ally | Devotion and competence extend the hero's reach; "mission support" becomes sacred service. |
| **Sancho Panza** | Don Quixote (1605) | Foil; grounder; comic relief; common-sense check | Operationalizes the ideal/real conflict. Madariaga analyzed reciprocal transformation: "Quixotization of Sancho and Sanchification of Quixote." |
| **Dr. Watson** | Sherlock Holmes | Narrator/biographer; colleague; caregiver | Shapes how audience values the hero; frames genius as socially legible. |
| **Samwise Gamgee** | Frodo (*Lord of the Rings*) | Emotional support; moral grounding; helper; endurance engine; "servant hero" | Tolkien called him "the chief hero." Loyalty and caretaking become heroic competencies. See deep dive below. |
| **Ron Weasley** | Harry Potter | Emotional support; comic relief; foil; grounding in wizarding-world normalcy | Provides Harry access to family and ordinariness he's never had. |
| **Chewbacca** | Han Solo (*Star Wars*) | Helper; competence extender; loyalty anchor | Physical protector whose life-debt embodies the companion's existential commitment. |

## Vogler on the Ally — Deep Dive

Vogler's settled term is **"Ally."** This is the heading of the chapter in *The Writer's Journey*. Within the chapter Vogler treats the following as overlapping or interchangeable terms for the same archetypal function: **Companion**, **Sidekick**, **Helpful servant**, **Sparring partner**, **Buddy**.

The key Vogler move is naming Ally as a **function** rather than a fixed character type. Provenance note: the original **1985 seven-page memo** does *not* contain Ally as a separate archetype — it discusses only stages, where "Tests, Allies, Enemies" is Stage 6. The eight-archetype framework is a development of the book.

### Dramatic Functions Vogler Assigns

- **Companion / sounding board.** Foundational function — gives the hero someone to talk to.
- **Sparring partner.** Han Solo's contrarianism with Luke is his canonical example.
- **Conscience.** External ethical mirror.
- **Comic relief.** Explicitly assigned to the Ally (overlapping with Trickster).
- **Errands, messages, scouting.** "It's useful to have someone to send on errands, to carry messages, to scout locations."
- **Humanizing the Hero.** Vogler's specific answer to the "stoic warrior" problem.
- **Audience surrogate / point-of-view character.** A new-to-the-Special-World Ally lets the writer deliver exposition naturally; Allies can also absorb sympathy when heroes do morally compromised things.
- **Expressing what the Hero cannot.** Fear, humor, ignorance, doubt.
- **Challenging the Hero / foil.** Without ceasing to be an Ally.
- **Holding things — literal and figurative.** Vogler's mythological example: **Iolaus**, who cauterizes the Hydra's necks while Hercules cuts off the heads.
- **Multiplication.** An Ally "may often appear as more than one person or animal." **Charlemagne's Paladins** are his classic multi-ally band.

### Psychological Function (the Jungian Layer)

This is the one place Vogler's treatment is genuinely original:

> The Ally represents **unexpressed, under-utilized, or forgotten parts of the Hero's personality** that need to come forward to complete the journey.

Vogler distinguishes Ally from Shadow:
- The **Shadow** is the *suppressed* or *repressed* dark/dangerous content of the psyche.
- The **Ally** is the *forgotten* or *neglected* positive content — warmth, humor, practicality, loyalty, common sense, earthiness.

The Hero's task is not to defeat or integrate the Ally (as with the Shadow) but to *recognize* and *rely* on these neglected resources.

### Vogler's Own Examples

- **Star Wars:** Han Solo (reluctant-buddy Ally), Chewbacca (silent-loyal Ally), R2-D2 and C-3PO (comic-relief Ally pair). Han is the proof that an Ally need not be friendly at first.
- **The Wizard of Oz:** Toto as Dorothy's earliest non-human Ally; Scarecrow/Tin Woodsman/Lion as an Ally band each personifying a quality Dorothy needs. Vogler's clearest illustration of the "Ally as forgotten part of the Hero's personality" thesis.
- **The Last of the Mohicans:** Chingachgook and Uncas as Allies to Hawkeye.
- **Hercules and Iolaus** — the chapter's mythological anchor.
- **Charlemagne and the Paladins** — Ally as fellowship.
- **Zorro and Bernardo** — the helpful-servant variant.
- **Prince Hal and Falstaff** — Ally as also Trickster.
- **Pulp Fiction:** Vincent and Jules as co-heroes who function as Allies for each other.
- **The Lion King:** Timon and Pumbaa as comic-relief Allies who also serve as substitute family.
- **Beverly Hills Cop, Casablanca:** Vogler's "watering hole" thesis — the cantina/saloon/Rick's Café as the canonical Ally-acquisition setting.

### Where the Ally Lives in the 12 Stages

- **Ordinary World / Call:** Allies sometimes pre-exist the journey; may even become the Herald.
- **Crossing the First Threshold:** First Ally often met at the threshold (Han Solo for Luke).
- **Stage 6 — Tests, Allies, Enemies:** The Ally's home stage. The "watering hole" scene lets the Hero "observe people under pressure, when true character is revealed."
- **Approach to the Inmost Cave:** Allies prepare alongside the Hero.
- **Ordeal:** Allies often temporarily abandoned (Hero faces death alone) or sacrificed — one of the most powerful Act-Two-into-Act-Three engines.
- **Reward / Road Back / Resurrection:** Allies celebrate, then help escape; final victory still belongs to the Hero.
- **Return with the Elixir:** Allies become witnesses and beneficiaries.

Nuance: Act One Allies are **functional/situational**, Act Two Allies become **structural**, Act Three Allies become **symbolic**.

### Archetype Crossovers

Vogler treats archetype-switching as not just possible but expected for Allies — *because the Ally function is the most loosely defined*:

- **Ally → Mentor.** Alfred Pennyworth; Han Solo mentoring Luke in space tactics.
- **Mentor → Ally.** A Mentor descending into the journey alongside the Hero.
- **Threshold Guardian → Ally.** Hostile gatekeeper flips into Ally once Hero proves worthy.
- **Ally → Shapeshifter.** When loyalty becomes uncertain. Gollum's whole arc.
- **Ally → Trickster.** The comic-relief Ally borrows the Trickster's tools (mischief, irreverence, ego-puncturing humor) without abandoning loyalty.

### Vogler's Advice to Writers

- **Pitfall — exposition vehicle only.** Give Allies their own opinions, arc, and ideally subplot.
- **Pitfall — Ally as projection of writer's affection.** An Ally needs the same character-design rigor as a Hero.
- **Use multiple Allies for distinct functions.** Don't load all functions onto one character. The Wizard of Oz trio is the template.
- **Don't let the Ally do the Hero's job.** "While Allies may provide support, the ultimate victory must be the Hero's."
- **Consider non-human allies.** Animals, spirits, AIs, ghosts, imaginary friends.
- **Use the Ally to introduce the Special World.** Ally-new-to-the-world asks audience questions; Ally-native explains rules.
- **Use Ally death sparingly but decisively.** Should propel transformation, not just generate sentiment.

### Limitations of Vogler's Treatment

Several specific weaknesses have been noted:

- **The chapter is short.** Among the briefest archetype chapters in the book — significantly shorter than Hero, Mentor, or Shadow.
- **The Ally is treated as "hired help."** Critics note Vogler frames Allies as story-machinery rather than as full subjects.
- **The category is too elastic.** Almost any non-hostile character qualifies, draining the term of analytic power.
- **Thin psychological function.** The "forgotten parts of the personality" gloss is suggestive but underdeveloped compared to Vogler's treatment of Hero, Shadow, or Mentor.
- **Gender / cultural calibration.** Critics note Vogler's Allies are often calibrated to a male-hero paradigm (the buddy, the sidekick), under-considering relational and care-based companion archetypes central to women's stories.
- **No Enkidu, no Patroclus, no Sam.** Vogler doesn't discuss any of the three deepest companion case studies in Western literature at any length. *The Lord of the Rings* is referenced throughout the book, but the Ally chapter does not pause for a sustained Sam analysis.

## Samwise Gamgee — Deep Dive

Sam is the companion archetype's **definitive expression** — sitting at the intersection of squire tradition, class-coded servanthood, and heroic transformation, while remaining narratively "ordinary."

### Tolkien's Own Words

**Letter 131** (to Milton Waldman, c. 1951):

> "I think the simple 'rustic' love of Sam and his Rosie (nowhere elaborated) is absolutely essential to the study of his (the chief hero's) character, and to the theme of the relation of ordinary life (breathing, eating, working, begetting) and quests, sacrifice, causes, and the 'longing for Elves', and sheer beauty."

> **⚠️ Cross-check note:** One AI source interprets "(the chief hero's)" as referring to Aragorn rather than Sam. The dominant scholarly consensus — supported by the Tolkien Forum, the One Wiki to Rule Them All, multiple academic theses, and the Rebuilding Hollin close-reading — interprets the parenthetical as Tolkien clarifying that **Sam IS the chief hero**. The parentheses are Tolkien's own (not editorial insertions). Additionally, Tolkien's broader context in the same letter — discussing how "without the simple and ordinary the noble and heroic is meaningless" — supports Sam as the referent.

**Letter to a real Sam Gamgee** (1956):

> "the 'Sam Gamgee' of my story is a most heroic character, now widely beloved by many readers, even though his origins are rustic."

**Letter 246** (to Mrs. Eileen Elgar, September 1963):

> "Sam was cocksure, and deep down a little conceited; but his conceit had been transformed by his devotion to Frodo. He did not think of himself as heroic or even brave, or in any way admirable — except in his service and loyalty to his master. That had an ingredient (probably inevitable) of pride and possessiveness: it is difficult to exclude it from the devotion of those who perform such service. In any case it prevented him from fully understanding the master that he loved, and from following him in his gradual education to the nobility of service to the unlovable and of perception of damaged good in the corrupt."

### The Batman Connection

Tolkien described Sam as "a reflexion of the English soldier, of the privates and batmen I knew in the 1914 War, and recognized as so far superior to myself."

In WWI, a **batman** was a soldier assigned to an officer for personal service — cooking, carrying kit, maintaining equipment — while still expected to fight. Scholar John Garth analyzed this extensively: the Frodo-Sam relationship reflects the hierarchy of officer and servant. Sam addresses Frodo as "Mr. Frodo" and "Master" throughout.

But through prolonged peril, the hierarchy gradually inverts — Frodo moves toward childlike dependency while Sam provides solutions — mirroring how working-class privates often possessed more practical wisdom than their university-educated officers.

### Sam's Own Hero's Journey

| Campbell Stage | Sam's Experience |
|---------------|-----------------|
| **Ordinary World** | Gardener in the Shire; simple, contented life tending plants |
| **Call to Adventure** | Overheard eavesdropping on Gandalf discussing the Ring; conscripted. Also motivated by a longing to "see Elves" |
| **Crossing the Threshold** | Leaving the Shire for the first time — the farthest from home he's ever been |
| **Road of Trials** | The Dead Marshes, Shelob's Lair, the desolation of Mordor |
| **Ordeal / "Belly of the Whale"** | Shelob's attack. Believing Frodo dead, Sam must decide to carry the Ring and continue alone — a profound psychological shift into independent heroism |
| **Apotheosis / Rebirth** | Carrying Frodo up Mount Doom: "I can't carry it for you, but I can carry you." Death and rebirth alongside Frodo |
| **Return with the Elixir** | Returns to the Shire with Galadriel's box of blessed earth and a Mallorn seed. Restores the devastated Shire. Marries Rosie Cotton. Serves as Mayor for seven consecutive seven-year terms |

### Symbolism

**The Gardener:** Galadriel's gift — a box of blessed earth with a Mallorn seed — carries Edenic resonance. Fleming Rutledge interprets this as parallel to Genesis. Sam uses it to restore the Shire after Saruman's devastation. His name itself encodes humility: the Westron "Banazîr" translates to "halfwise" or "simple."

**The Ring's Temptation:** Sam is offered visions of becoming "Samwise the Strong, Hero of the Age," transforming the world into a vast garden. His hobbit-sense rejects the fantasy — he doesn't need a kingdom, only a humble patch of good earth. Tom Shippey notes Sam's particular courage is the ability to "be cheerful without any hope at all."

**Christian Parallels:** Sam carrying Frodo up Mount Doom parallels Simon of Cyrene carrying Christ's cross to Golgotha. The Ring's destruction on March 25 — the Feast of the Annunciation — was Tolkien's deliberate choice. Ralph Wood concludes that Frodo and Sam are "first in the reign of Ilúvatar because they are willing to be last and least."

**The Final Line:** The story's last line belongs not to Frodo but to Sam, returning home from the Grey Havens: "Well, I'm back." The narrative cannot end until the companion's journey is complete.

### Sam as a Test of Vogler's Ally Category

Sam ticks every Voglerian Ally box — companion/sounding board, conscience, comic relief, scout, humanizer, audience surrogate, forgotten parts of the hero's personality. But Sam **exceeds** Vogler's Ally in three ways:

- **He becomes the actual carrier.** Sam carries Frodo up Mount Doom, briefly carries the Ring, and survives the destruction of the Ring with his moral identity intact. Vogler's Ally is supposed to *enable* the Hero's victory, not partly *substitute* for it.
- **His arc is full and independent.** Sam returns, marries Rosie, becomes Mayor seven times, inherits the Red Book. He runs a complete Hero's Journey in parallel — exactly the kind of Ally arc Vogler advises writers to create but does not develop in depth.
- **His function is sacramental, not utilitarian.** Sam's relation to Frodo is closer to Patroclus-Achilles or Enkidu-Gilgamesh — a soul-bond mythologem older than the Western sidekick — than to Han Solo or Tonto.

Sam is the Ally that **proves Vogler's critics right** — that the archetype deserved more pages than it got.

## Sancho Panza — The Pragmatic Companion

If Sam represents the **sacrificial companion**, Sancho Panza represents the **pragmatic companion**. In Cervantes's *Don Quixote* (1605), Sancho follows his "visionary master" out of a mixture of greed, curiosity, and loyalty — motivated by the promised reward of an island governorship.

Sancho is the novel's only character who exists **both inside and outside Quixote's "mad world."** He acts as a foil: where Quixote is serious and reliant on fantasy, Sancho is humorous and grounded in common sense.

**Reciprocal transformation** (per scholar Salvador de Madariaga): the "Quixotization of Sancho and the Sanchification of Quixote." By the novel's end, it is the practical squire who begs the dying knight to continue their adventures. Sancho evolves from an illiterate farmer into a wise and just ruler during his actual governorship — proving that leadership is not exclusive to the educated aristocracy.

## The Companion and Hierarchy — Scholarly Critique

Recent scholarship emphasizes sidekicks frequently encode class, race, and hierarchy dynamics. The squire role can either reproduce or critique hierarchy, depending on whether servanthood is romanticized, problematized, or transformed into equal partnership.

The hierarchy question is especially live for Sam: is the Frodo-Sam relationship a celebration of working-class moral superiority, or a romanticization of class-based servitude? Tolkien himself was ambivalent. In Letter 246 he explicitly named the pride and possessiveness "probably inevitable" in such devotion — a remarkably clear-eyed admission that the squire archetype carries an ideological cost.

A 2024 special edition of *Media Practice and Education* called for moving beyond Campbell's individualistic monomyth toward "narrative plurality" — frameworks that take companions, ensembles, and distributed agency as primary rather than secondary.

## Synthesis: Heroism as Distributed

The Hero's Journey frameworks all describe transformation through ordeal. But the companion archetype reveals a deeper truth: **the hero's journey is never truly solo**.

Campbell's "helpers" along the Road of Trials, Vogler's entire sixth stage devoted to forging alliances, Harmon's implicit reliance on relationship as the vehicle for change — all acknowledge that transformation requires witness, support, and sacrifice from others.

The debate over whether the companion is the "true hero" misses the more interesting insight. Tolkien's genius was not in making Sam secretly more important than Frodo but in demonstrating that **heroism is distributed across relationships**:

- Frodo carries the Ring; Sam carries Frodo
- Frodo endures spiritual corruption; Sam endures watching someone he loves be destroyed
- Frodo fails at the final moment; Sam succeeds in getting him there
- Neither journey has meaning without the other

This is precisely what Tolkien meant: **"Without the high and noble the simple and vulgar is utterly mean; and without the simple and ordinary the noble and heroic is meaningless."**

From Enkidu refusing to let Gilgamesh face Humbaba alone, to Sancho Panza begging a dying Quixote to ride again, to Sam Gamgee lifting Frodo onto his back on the slopes of Mount Doom — the companion is not a supporting character in the hero's story. **The companion is the story's argument that no one is saved alone.**

This is the addition that the standard frameworks underspecify. Campbell, Vogler, Harmon, and Murdock all center the protagonist. They are right to — most stories have one. But the companion is not merely a character in the hero's story; the companion is **the structural acknowledgment that the hero's transformation requires another consciousness to witness, support, and sometimes complete it**. A framework that treats the companion as an afterthought treats the journey as more solitary than journeys actually are.
